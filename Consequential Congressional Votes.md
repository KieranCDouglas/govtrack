# Most Consequential Congressional Votes: 114th–118th Congress

Each list ranks the 10 most consequential votes cast in that chamber during that Congress, based on policy significance, historical weight, long-term impact, and the degree to which the vote represented a genuine choice point for the country. Votes on legislation that passed in both chambers are noted under the chamber that cast the pivotal or first vote; the companion chamber's vote is noted in the description. Vote tallies and dates are sourced from official Senate and House roll call records.

---

## 114th Congress (Jan. 3, 2015 – Jan. 3, 2017)
*Context: Republicans held a 247-seat House majority and a 54-seat Senate majority, facing a Democratic White House under President Obama. The defining tension was a GOP Congress testing the limits of its mandate against presidential vetoes.*

---

### House

| # | Vote | Date | Tally | Result |
|---|------|------|-------|--------|
| 1 | **ACA Reconciliation Repeal (H.R. 3762)** — First-ever passage of an ACA repeal bill through both chambers via reconciliation, forcing Obama to exercise a veto. Defunded Planned Parenthood and repealed the individual mandate, employer mandate, and Cadillac tax. | Jan. 6, 2016 | 240–181 | Passed |
| 2 | **Keystone XL Pipeline Approval Act (S. 1)** — Authorized construction of the Keystone XL pipeline, bypassing the State Dept. review process. Obama vetoed it; the House override attempt failed 270–152 (short of 2/3). A landmark congressional assertion of authority over executive energy permitting. | Feb. 11, 2015 | 270–152 | Passed (veto override failed) |
| 3 | **USA FREEDOM Act (H.R. 2048)** — Ended the NSA's bulk collection of domestic phone metadata under Section 215 of the PATRIOT Act, replacing it with a targeted collection program. The most significant surveillance reform since the PATRIOT Act's passage. | May 13, 2015 | 338–88 | Passed |
| 4 | **Every Student Succeeds Act (S. 1177)** — Reauthorized the Elementary and Secondary Education Act, replacing No Child Left Behind and substantially rolling back federal control of K–12 education. Represented the largest devolution of federal education authority in a generation. | Dec. 2, 2015 | 359–64 | Passed |
| 5 | **FAST Act — Long-Term Highway Reauthorization (H.R. 22)** — The first long-term (5-year) surface transportation bill in over a decade, authorizing $305 billion for highway, transit, rail, and safety programs. Ended the cycle of temporary extensions that had stalled infrastructure investment. | Nov. 5, 2015 | 363–64 | Passed |
| 6 | **Trade Promotion Authority (TPA) (H.R. 1314)** — Granted the President fast-track authority to negotiate major trade deals (principally the Trans-Pacific Partnership) subject only to an up-or-down congressional vote. Passed over the opposition of most House Democrats, including Minority Leader Pelosi. | Jun. 18, 2015 | 218–208 | Passed |
| 7 | **Bipartisan Budget Act of 2015 (H.R. 1314)** — Suspended the debt ceiling through March 2017, raised sequester spending caps by $80 billion over two years, and reformed Social Security disability and retirement claiming strategies. Prevented a default and ended a major fiscal confrontation. | Oct. 28, 2015 | 266–167 | Passed |
| 8 | **21st Century Cures Act (H.R. 34)** — Accelerated FDA drug and device approval, injected $4.8 billion into NIH, funded the BRAIN Initiative, and included mental health reforms. Passed with rare overwhelming bipartisan support near the end of the Congress. | Nov. 30, 2016 | 392–26 | Passed |
| 9 | **Justice Against Sponsors of Terrorism Act (JASTA) (S. 2040)** — Allowed 9/11 families to sue the Saudi government in U.S. federal courts. Congress overrode Obama's veto 348–77 in the House — the only successful veto override of the 114th Congress and the only one to name a foreign government as a defendant. | Sep. 28, 2016 | 348–77 | Veto overridden |
| 10 | **Iran Nuclear Agreement Review Act (H.R. 1191)** — Required Congress to review, and potentially disapprove, the Iran nuclear deal (JCPOA) before the President could waive sanctions. Although the Senate blocked the disapproval resolution via filibuster, the act established an unprecedented congressional check on executive nuclear diplomacy. | May 14, 2015 | 400–25 | Passed |

### Senate

| # | Vote | Date | Tally | Result |
|---|------|------|-------|--------|
| 1 | **ACA Reconciliation Repeal (H.R. 3762)** — Senate passage of the ACA repeal bill was the pivotal vote: using the reconciliation process for the first time to target the ACA, it sent the bill to Obama's desk for the first time. The 52–47 vote along near-party lines defined Republican health care strategy for years. | Dec. 3, 2015 | 52–47 | Passed |
| 2 | **Iran Nuclear Agreement Review Act (H.R. 1191)** — Passed 98–1, this law required a congressional review period before Obama could lift sanctions under the JCPOA. It then set up the pivotal September 2015 filibuster vote, where Senate Democrats blocked the disapproval resolution (58–42, falling short of 60), effectively allowing the Iran deal to take effect. The framework redefined presidential treaty powers. | May 7, 2015 | 98–1 | Passed |
| 3 | **Trade Promotion Authority (TPA) (H.R. 2146)** — The Senate passed TPA 60–38 after a rare procedural battle that split the Democratic caucus and required two separate cloture votes. The vote opened the path to TPP negotiations and was the most consequential trade vote since NAFTA. | Jun. 24, 2015 | 60–38 | Passed |
| 4 | **Every Student Succeeds Act (Conference Report, S. 1177)** — Senate passage of ESSA 85–12 capped a genuine bipartisan achievement, replacing a deeply unpopular law and establishing a new, decentralized framework for federal education policy. | Dec. 9, 2015 | 85–12 | Passed |
| 5 | **Keystone XL Override Attempt** — The Senate voted 62–36 to override Obama's Keystone veto, falling four votes short of the two-thirds needed. The failed override vote definitively closed the Keystone chapter and cemented the limits of Republican energy authority. | Mar. 4, 2015 | 62–36 | Override failed |
| 6 | **JASTA Veto Override** — The Senate voted 97–1 to override Obama's veto of JASTA — the most lopsided veto override vote in modern history and the only one of the Congress. It established that U.S. courts could exercise jurisdiction over foreign sovereigns in terrorism cases. | Sep. 28, 2016 | 97–1 | Veto overridden |
| 7 | **USA FREEDOM Act (H.R. 2048)** — After a dramatic lapse in Section 215 authority and a rare floor debate on surveillance, the Senate passed the USA FREEDOM Act 67–32, curtailing bulk phone metadata collection. Senator Rand Paul's procedural blocking efforts drew national attention to civil liberties issues. | Jun. 2, 2015 | 67–32 | Passed |
| 8 | **Bipartisan Budget Act of 2015** — The Senate passed the debt-limit/spending-cap deal 64–35, averting default and funding the government through the end of the Obama presidency. McConnell's ability to secure 64 votes — a supermajority — demonstrated the ceiling of bipartisan cooperation available in this Congress. | Oct. 30, 2015 | 64–35 | Passed |
| 9 | **21st Century Cures Act** — The Senate passed the Cures Act 94–5, a bipartisan landmark that addressed the FDA's drug/device pipeline, mental health parity, and NIH funding. One of the most consequential health legislation achievements of the decade. | Dec. 7, 2016 | 94–5 | Passed |
| 10 | **Comprehensive Addiction and Recovery Act (CARA)** — Passed 92–2, this was the most sweeping drug legislation in decades, authorizing new prevention, treatment, and recovery programs to address the opioid crisis — then in its acute phase. Its passage reflected a rare, lopsided consensus that opioid addiction had become a national emergency. | Jul. 13, 2016 | 92–2 | Passed |

## 115th Congress (Jan. 3, 2017 – Jan. 3, 2019)
*Context: Republicans held unified control — 241 House seats, 51 Senate seats (later 52 after the Alabama special election), and the White House under President Trump. Characterized by failed ACA repeal attempts, a sweeping tax overhaul, and contentious judicial confirmations.*

---

### House

| # | Vote | Date | Tally | Result |
|---|------|------|-------|--------|
| 1 | **Tax Cuts and Jobs Act (H.R. 1)** — The largest overhaul of the U.S. tax code since 1986, cutting the corporate rate from 35% to 21% permanently, reducing individual rates temporarily, doubling the standard deduction, capping the SALT deduction at $10,000, repealing the ACA individual mandate, and opening ANWR to drilling. Cost approximately $1.5 trillion over 10 years. | Nov. 16, 2017 | 227–205 | Passed |
| 2 | **American Health Care Act (AHCA) (H.R. 1628)** — The House finally passed its ACA replacement after a failed first attempt in March 2017, phasing out Medicaid expansion, replacing subsidies with tax credits, and allowing states to waive essential health benefits. The bill died in the Senate but defined the central legislative battle of the Congress. | May 4, 2017 | 217–213 | Passed |
| 3 | **Countering America's Adversaries Through Sanctions Act (CAATSA) (H.R. 3364)** — Codified and strengthened sanctions on Russia, Iran, and North Korea into statute, stripping the President of unilateral authority to ease Russia sanctions. A rare bipartisan rebuke of the executive branch on foreign policy. | Jul. 25, 2017 | 419–3 | Passed |
| 4 | **First Step Act (S. 756)** — The most significant federal criminal justice reform in decades, reducing mandatory minimum sentences, expanding early release provisions, and directing rehabilitative programming. Unusual in that it was championed by a Republican president and a bipartisan House majority. | Dec. 20, 2018 | 358–36 | Passed |
| 5 | **Bipartisan Budget Act of 2018 (H.R. 1892)** — Raised discretionary spending caps by $296 billion over two years ($165B defense, $131B domestic), suspended the debt ceiling through March 2019, and extended government funding. The deal effectively ended the Budget Control Act's spending limits. | Feb. 9, 2018 | 240–186 | Passed |
| 6 | **Agriculture Improvement Act of 2018 / Farm Bill (H.R. 2)** — Reauthorized all major federal farm and nutrition programs for five years, including a major legalization of industrial hemp. Retained SNAP eligibility broadly, defeating conservative work-requirement proposals that had initially passed the House. | Dec. 12, 2018 | 369–47 | Passed |
| 7 | **Consolidated Appropriations Act, 2018 (H.R. 1625)** — A $1.3 trillion omnibus appropriations bill that funded the entire government, the largest single-year spending bill in history at that time. Trump's public complaint after signing set the stage for later shutdown confrontations. | Mar. 22, 2018 | 256–167 | Passed |
| 8 | **S. 2155 — Economic Growth, Regulatory Relief, and Consumer Protection Act** — The first rollback of Dodd-Frank since its 2010 enactment, raising the bank stress-test threshold from $50B to $250B and relieving community banks from mortgage-underwriting rules. Passed with unusual bipartisan support. | May 22, 2018 | 258–159 | Passed |
| 9 | **FY2018 National Defense Authorization Act (H.R. 2810)** — Authorized $626 billion for defense, including the largest military pay raise in years, creation of a Space Command, and new authorities to counter Russian hybrid warfare. Passed with overwhelming bipartisan support and represented a major expansion of the defense budget. | Jul. 14, 2017 | 344–81 | Passed |
| 10 | **S.J. Res. 54 — Yemen War Powers Resolution** — The House passed a resolution directing the removal of U.S. forces from the Saudi-led Yemen intervention not authorized by Congress. Though vetoed by Trump, it was the first invocation of the War Powers Resolution to pass both chambers since its 1973 enactment. | Feb. 13, 2019 | 248–177 | Passed (vetoed) |

---

### Senate

| # | Vote | Date | Tally | Result |
|---|------|------|-------|--------|
| 1 | **Tax Cuts and Jobs Act (H.R. 1)** — The Senate passed TCJA 51–48 on a strict party-line vote (Bob Corker the sole Republican no). The vote permanently reshaped corporate taxation and was the single largest legislative achievement of the Trump administration's first term. | Dec. 19, 2017 | 51–48 | Passed |
| 2 | **"Skinny Repeal" of ACA (Health Care Freedom Act) — Vote-a-Rama Final Amendment** — In the decisive moment of years of ACA repeal effort, Senators John McCain, Susan Collins, and Lisa Murkowski joined all Democrats to reject the narrowest ACA repeal 49–51. McCain's dramatic thumbs-down became the defining image of the Congress. | Jul. 28, 2017 | 49–51 | Rejected |
| 3 | **Confirmation of Neil Gorsuch to the Supreme Court** — McConnell deployed the "nuclear option," eliminating the 60-vote cloture threshold for Supreme Court nominees and confirming Gorsuch 54–45. The rules change permanently altered the Senate's confirmation dynamics and its relationship to judicial independence. | Apr. 7, 2017 | 54–45 | Confirmed |
| 4 | **Confirmation of Brett Kavanaugh to the Supreme Court** — After one of the most contentious confirmation hearings in modern history, Kavanaugh was confirmed 50–48 — the narrowest Supreme Court confirmation since 1881. The vote, following allegations of sexual assault, became a defining cultural flashpoint. | Oct. 6, 2018 | 50–48 | Confirmed |
| 5 | **CAATSA (H.R. 3364) — Russia/Iran/North Korea Sanctions** — The Senate passed CAATSA 98–2, an extraordinary bipartisan rebuke that codified Russia sanctions into statute and constrained the President's waiver authority. Directly responded to the intelligence community's findings on Russian election interference. | Jul. 27, 2017 | 98–2 | Passed |
| 6 | **Bipartisan Budget Act of 2018 (H.R. 1892)** — Passed 71–28 with heavy bipartisan support, the Budget Act broke the sequester spending caps and ended the era of austerity budgeting that had defined Congress since 2011. A brief government shutdown occurred before the bill cleared, when Sen. Rand Paul objected to the deficit impact. | Feb. 9, 2018 | 71–28 | Passed |
| 7 | **S.J. Res. 54 — Yemen War Powers Resolution** — The Senate passed the Yemen resolution 56–41, the first-ever Senate passage of a War Powers Resolution. Seven Republicans crossed the aisle, reflecting growing bipartisan unease over the Saudi relationship and undeclared military commitments. | Dec. 13, 2018 | 56–41 | Passed (vetoed) |
| 8 | **First Step Act (S. 756)** — Passed 87–12, the bill achieved a historically rare bipartisan consensus on criminal justice reform, reducing minimum mandatory sentences and creating pathways to earlier release. Supported by the White House, the Koch network, and the NAACP simultaneously. | Dec. 18, 2018 | 87–12 | Passed |
| 9 | **FY2018 National Defense Authorization Act** — Passed 89–8, authorizing a major buildup in defense spending and establishing the Space Force as a new branch. The consistent, lopsided margins for the NDAA contrast sharply with the partisan breakdown on most other legislation. | Nov. 16, 2017 | 89–8 | Passed |
| 10 | **Budget Resolution (H.Con.Res. 71) — Unlocking Tax Reconciliation** — The Senate passed the budget resolution 51–49 — a purely procedural vote, but one of the most consequential of the Congress, as it opened the reconciliation pathway that made the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act possible without the 60-vote threshold. | Oct. 19, 2017 | 51–49 | Passed |

## 116th Congress (Jan. 3, 2019 – Jan. 3, 2021)
*Context: Democrats recaptured the House (235 seats) while Republicans retained the Senate (53 seats). The dominant themes were oversight confrontations with President Trump, two impeachment articles, and an unprecedented pandemic response.*

---

### House

| # | Vote | Date | Tally | Result |
|---|------|------|-------|--------|
| 1 | **First Impeachment of Donald Trump (H.Res. 755)** — The House voted to impeach President Trump on two articles — Abuse of Power and Obstruction of Congress — arising from the Ukraine phone call and obstruction of the congressional inquiry. The third presidential impeachment in U.S. history. | Dec. 18, 2019 | 230–197 (Art. I); 229–198 (Art. II) | Impeached |
| 2 | **CARES Act (H.R. 748)** — A $2.2 trillion emergency relief package — the largest in U.S. history at that point — providing $1,200 direct payments, $600/week expanded unemployment insurance, $349 billion in small business PPP loans, and $500 billion in corporate stabilization funds. Passed the House by voice vote under unanimous-consent agreement. | Mar. 27, 2020 | Voice vote | Passed |
| 3 | **HEROES Act (H.R. 6800)** — A $3 trillion follow-on relief package passed by the Democratic House in May 2020. Though it died in the Senate, it established the framework for subsequent negotiations and directly influenced the $900 billion December 2020 relief bill. The largest bill passed by either chamber in history. | May 15, 2020 | 208–199 | Passed |
| 4 | **H.J.Res. 46 — Blocking Trump's Border Wall Emergency Declaration** — The House voted to overturn Trump's declaration of a national emergency to redirect military funds to border wall construction — the first time Congress moved to terminate a presidential emergency declaration. Trump vetoed it; the House override attempt failed 248–181. | Feb. 26, 2019 | 245–182 | Passed (vetoed) |
| 5 | **USMCA Implementation Act (H.R. 5430)** — The House passed the United States–Mexico–Canada Agreement, replacing NAFTA with a modernized trade framework including stronger labor standards, digital trade rules, and automotive content requirements. The deal was a bipartisan accomplishment, with the AFL-CIO's support. | Dec. 19, 2019 | 385–41 | Passed |
| 6 | **George Floyd Justice in Policing Act (H.R. 7120)** — Passed amid the 2020 racial justice uprising, the bill would have banned chokeholds, ended qualified immunity for police, required federal officers to wear body cameras, and created a national misconduct registry. It passed the House but died in the Senate, defining the limits of police reform. | Jun. 25, 2020 | 236–181 | Passed (died in Senate) |
| 7 | **Consolidated Appropriations Act, 2021 / COVID Relief (H.R. 133)** — Combined a $1.4 trillion omnibus with a $900 billion COVID relief package, including a second round of $600 stimulus checks, extended unemployment insurance, and renewed PPP funding. | Dec. 21, 2020 | 359–53 | Passed |
| 8 | **S.J.Res. 7 — Yemen War Powers Resolution** — Passed for the second time in consecutive Congresses, invoking the War Powers Act to end U.S. support for the Saudi coalition in Yemen. Vetoed by Trump. Demonstrated sustained congressional pushback on executive war powers. | Apr. 4, 2019 | 247–175 | Passed (vetoed) |
| 9 | **For the People Act (H.R. 1)** — Sweeping voting rights, campaign finance, and ethics overhaul: automatic voter registration, independent redistricting commissions, expansion of mail-in voting, and mandatory presidential tax return disclosure. Passed the House but never came to a floor vote in the Senate under McConnell. | Mar. 8, 2019 | 234–193 | Passed (died in Senate) |
| 10 | **Bipartisan Budget Act of 2019 (H.R. 3877)** — Suspended the debt ceiling through July 2021 and raised spending caps by $320 billion over two years. Though relatively routine in structure, its passage — at a critical moment when the U.S. faced debt-limit risks — was essential to keeping the government funded through the pandemic period. | Jul. 25, 2019 | 284–149 | Passed |

---

### Senate

| # | Vote | Date | Tally | Result |
|---|------|------|-------|--------|
| 1 | **First Impeachment Trial of Donald Trump — Article I: Abuse of Power** — Senators voted 52–48 to acquit Trump on the abuse of power article, with Mitt Romney the only Republican to vote guilty — the first senator in history to vote to convict a president of his own party in an impeachment trial. | Feb. 5, 2020 | 52 Not Guilty / 48 Guilty | Acquitted |
| 2 | **CARES Act (H.R. 748)** — The Senate first passed this unprecedented $2.2 trillion relief bill 96–0, the largest emergency economic legislation in American history, providing direct payments, expanded unemployment, business loans, and hospital funding. | Mar. 25, 2020 | 96–0 | Passed |
| 3 | **Consolidated Appropriations Act, 2021 / COVID Relief (H.R. 133)** — Combined the FY2021 omnibus with a $900 billion COVID relief package. The Senate passed it 92–6 after a procedural stand-off with President Trump, who had demanded larger direct payments. | Dec. 21, 2020 | 92–6 | Passed |
| 4 | **USMCA Implementation Act (H.R. 5430)** — The Senate passed the NAFTA replacement 89–10. For the first time in decades, a major trade agreement earned support from labor unions — reflected in broader Senate approval margins. | Jan. 16, 2020 | 89–10 | Passed |
| 5 | **Confirmation of Amy Coney Barrett to the Supreme Court** — Barrett's confirmation 52–48 — with no Democratic votes — made her the third Trump Supreme Court justice and secured a 6-3 conservative majority. The vote occurred eight days before the presidential election, fulfilling McConnell's reversal of his own "election year" standard from 2016. | Oct. 26, 2020 | 52–48 | Confirmed |
| 6 | **H.J.Res. 46 — Blocking the Border Wall Emergency Declaration** — The Senate voted 59–41 to block Trump's emergency declaration, with 12 Republicans joining all Democrats. Trump vetoed it; the Senate's 59-vote support for override fell short of 67 needed. The vote showed the limits of Republican deference to presidential emergency powers. | Mar. 14, 2019 | 59–41 | Passed (vetoed; override failed) |
| 7 | **S.J.Res. 7 — Yemen War Powers Resolution** — First passage of a War Powers resolution through the Senate 54–46, including a second vote 56–41 in the next session. Represented the broadest bipartisan Senate coalition ever assembled against an ongoing military operation. | Apr. 4, 2019 | 54–46 | Passed (vetoed) |
| 8 | **Bipartisan Budget Act of 2019 (H.R. 3877)** — Passed 67–28, suspending the debt ceiling through July 2021 and raising spending caps. The two-year suspension was essential because it kept the country solvent through the onset of the COVID pandemic, when emergency spending would otherwise have immediately triggered a default crisis. | Aug. 1, 2019 | 67–28 | Passed |
| 9 | **Great American Outdoors Act (S. 3422)** — Permanently fully funded the Land and Water Conservation Fund ($900M/year) and authorized $9.5 billion over five years for deferred maintenance in national parks. A rare, significant bipartisan environmental achievement in a highly polarized era. | Jun. 17, 2020 | 73–25 | Passed |
| 10 | **COVID-19 Emergency Paid Leave / Families First Act (H.R. 6201)** — The Senate passed the first mandatory paid sick leave law in federal history — requiring two weeks of paid leave for COVID-affected workers in businesses under 500 employees — 90–8. Though limited in scope, it was a structural precedent with lasting policy implications. | Mar. 17, 2020 | 90–8 | Passed |

## 117th Congress (Jan. 3, 2021 – Jan. 3, 2023)
*Context: Democrats controlled both chambers narrowly — a 222-seat House majority and a 50-50 Senate (with Vice President Harris as tiebreaker). Defined by one of the most productive legislative periods in decades, passing major economic, climate, infrastructure, and gun safety laws.*

---

### House

| # | Vote | Date | Tally | Result |
|---|------|------|-------|--------|
| 1 | **Second Impeachment of Donald Trump (H.Res. 24)** — Ten House Republicans joined all Democrats to impeach Trump for "incitement of insurrection" following the January 6 Capitol attack — the most bipartisan presidential impeachment in history and the first impeachment of a president twice. | Jan. 13, 2021 | 232–197 | Impeached |
| 2 | **American Rescue Plan Act (H.R. 1319)** — $1.9 trillion COVID relief, including $1,400 direct payments, $300/week extended unemployment, $350 billion to state/local governments, expanded child tax credit ($3,000–$3,600/child), and school reopening funds. Passed along strict party lines. | Feb. 27, 2021 | 219–212 | Passed |
| 3 | **Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (H.R. 3684)** — $1.2 trillion in physical infrastructure investment (roads, bridges, broadband, water systems, EV charging, passenger rail). The largest infrastructure bill since the Eisenhower interstate highway program. Passed with 13 House Republican votes. | Nov. 5, 2021 | 228–206 | Passed |
| 4 | **Inflation Reduction Act (H.R. 5376)** — $738 billion legislation addressing climate (the largest U.S. climate investment ever: ~$370 billion), health care (Medicare drug price negotiation, ACA subsidy extensions), and deficit reduction (~$238 billion). Passed via reconciliation on strict party lines. | Aug. 12, 2022 | 220–207 | Passed |
| 5 | **Build Back Better Act (H.R. 5376)** — The House passed Biden's $1.75 trillion social policy bill (pre-Senate negotiation), encompassing universal pre-K, paid family leave, the largest Medicaid expansion since its founding, and major climate provisions. The bill died in the Senate due to opposition from Sen. Joe Manchin, reshaping the scope of the 117th Congress's agenda. | Nov. 19, 2021 | 220–213 | Passed (died in Senate) |
| 6 | **Bipartisan Safer Communities Act (H.R. 2471 as vehicle)** — First federal gun safety legislation in nearly 30 years: enhanced background checks for under-21 buyers, funds for red flag laws, closed the "boyfriend loophole" in domestic violence gun restrictions, and increased mental health funding. | Jun. 24, 2022 | 234–193 | Passed |
| 7 | **CHIPS and Science Act (H.R. 4346)** — $280 billion to restore U.S. semiconductor manufacturing and STEM research, including $52 billion in direct manufacturing subsidies and $200 billion in science funding. The most significant industrial policy legislation in decades, responding directly to China's strategic challenge. | Jul. 28, 2022 | 243–187 | Passed |
| 8 | **Electoral Count Reform Act (included in FY2023 Omnibus)** — Clarified that the Vice President's role in certifying electoral votes is purely ceremonial, raised the threshold for congressional objections to electoral votes, and strengthened procedures for counting electors. Directly addressed the mechanisms exploited on January 6, 2021. | Dec. 23, 2022 | 225–201 | Passed |
| 9 | **Respect for Marriage Act (H.R. 8404)** — Repealed the Defense of Marriage Act and federally codified same-sex and interracial marriages, protecting them against any future Supreme Court ruling. Passed with 47 Republican House votes. | Jul. 19, 2022 | 267–157 | Passed |
| 10 | **For the People Act / Freedom to Vote Act (H.R. 1 / S. 2747)** — The House passed this comprehensive voting rights and democracy reform package twice. The Senate's repeated failure to invoke cloture (50–50, falling short of 60) was itself consequential, effectively ending the push for federal election standardization and redistricting reform. | Mar. 3, 2021 | 220–210 | Passed (died in Senate) |

## 118th Congress (Jan. 3, 2023 – Jan. 3, 2025)
*Context: Republicans won a thin House majority (222 seats) while Democrats retained the Senate (48D + 3 independents caucusing with Democrats). Characterized by an historically dysfunctional House, a landmark debt-limit confrontation, foreign aid battles, and intense Speaker politics.*

---

### House

| # | Vote | Date | Tally | Result |
|---|------|------|-------|--------|
| 1 | **Election of Kevin McCarthy as Speaker (15th Ballot)** — McCarthy's election required 15 rounds of voting over four days — the most protracted Speaker election since 1859. The concessions he made to the House Freedom Caucus (single-member motion to vacate, etc.) directly enabled his own ouster nine months later and defined the dysfunctional character of the entire Congress. | Jan. 7, 2023 | 216–212 | Elected |
| 2 | **Fiscal Responsibility Act of 2023 / Debt Ceiling Deal (H.R. 3746)** — Suspended the debt ceiling through January 2025 in exchange for capping discretionary spending, rescinding unspent COVID funds, adding work requirements to SNAP for certain recipients, and accelerating permitting for energy projects. Averted the first sovereign default in U.S. history by two days. | May 31, 2023 | 314–117 | Passed |
| 3 | **National Security Act, 2024 / Ukraine–Israel–Taiwan Aid Package (H.R. 815)** — After months of stalemate, the House passed a $95 billion foreign aid package for Ukraine, Israel, and Taiwan, alongside a TikTok divestiture mandate. The 311-112 margin masked an unusual dynamic: Speaker Johnson needed mostly Democratic votes to pass it over the objections of his own right flank. | Apr. 20, 2024 | 311–112 | Passed |
| 4 | **Ouster of Speaker Kevin McCarthy (Motion to Vacate, H.Res. 757)** — Matt Gaetz's motion to vacate the chair succeeded 216–210, removing McCarthy — the first Speaker in history to be removed mid-term by his own chamber. The vote plunged the House into a three-week leadership vacuum and underscored the collapse of Republican institutional authority. | Oct. 3, 2023 | 216–210 | Speaker removed |
